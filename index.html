Air Quality Index in Malaysia

The Air Quality Index (AQI) measures air pollution levels across Malaysia's monitoring stations. It ranges from 0-500, with higher values indicating greater health concerns. Values are categorized as Good (0-50), Moderate (51-100), Unhealthy (101-200), Very Unhealthy (201-300), and Hazardous (301+).

Regional Variations in Air Quality

Understanding how geography and monsoon patterns shape Malaysia's air pollution

The Traffic-Pollution Connection

How Malaysia's busiest roads and vehicle emissions impact local air quality

South West Coast

Most affected by transboundary haze from Indonesian fires, carried by southwest monsoon winds across the Strait of Malacca. High urbanization worsens the impact, making this region Malaysia's pollution hotspot.

North West Coast

Dense population and industrial activities drive higher baseline pollution, especially in Pulau Pinang. During haze episodes, local emissions combine with transboundary smoke for a double impact.

East Coast

Protected by the Titiwangsa mountain range and cleaner oceanic winds. Generally maintains moderate air quality, though occasional spikes occur from local industrial and agricultural activities.

Borneo

Maintains the cleanest air due to low population density, fewer industries, and distance from major haze sources. Natural rainforests and favorable wind patterns help keep pollution levels low.

Traffic Hotspots Along the West Coast

Malaysia's 10 busiest roads form a continuous corridor along Peninsular Malaysia's west coast, from Penang through the Klang Valley to Johor. Major highways handle over 200,000 vehicles daily at peak segments. This traffic concentration directly correlates with elevated AQI levelsâ€”states facing transboundary haze also battle heavy vehicular emissions, creating a dual pollution burden that explains persistently poor air quality in western urban centers.

Motor Vehicles Drive Local Emissions

Motor vehicles dominate Malaysia's CO emissions, accounting for the vast majority of 2.07 million metric tonnes released in 2022. Industries and power stations contribute relatively minor shares. This vehicle-centric profile explains why urban centers maintain elevated baseline AQI even without regional haze. The message is clear: daily traffic on busy corridors generates significant local pollution, making transportation reform crucial for long-term air quality improvement.

Malaysia's Forest Coverage Trajectory

Malaysia's forest-to-land ratio reveals a dramatic environmental story. After bottoming out at 14.19% in 2007, forest coverage climbed steadily to peak at 14.92% by 2013â€”reflecting successful conservation efforts.
